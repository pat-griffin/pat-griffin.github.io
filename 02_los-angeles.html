Medical Emergence? Use a Drone

The Guardian

The capability to deliver time sensitive medical supplies as well as samples around rural areas is another great potential for drone technology. Though Los Angeles does not really face this problem, countries such as Haiti,  the Dominican Republic, and Lesotho have a huge opportunity to benefit from this technology. As discussed in the article, Lesotho has been using this technology to help with its AIDS epidemic. With the drones capable of transporting the samples at a more affecting rate than traditional means of transportation.  Unlike these rural areas cities such as Los Angeles take an unreliable amount of time to travel short distances do to the excessive amount of traffic, it made me start to wonder how much more effective would it be to carry these time sensitive samples by air. Could a potential of these drones encourage a system similar to bike sharing but for air transportation in a dense city.

Threat of the Automated Revolution

LA Times

Vanity Fair

Automation is probably the biggest threat to jobs we have ever faced. The Industrial Revolution automated the manufacturing industry, removing jobs from artisans. The Digital Revolution has so far automated communication as well as countless other tasks, but this has not yet reached its apex.  According to a report by PricewaterhouseCoopers “Thirty-eight percent of jobs in the U.S. are at high risk of being replaced by robots and artificial intelligence over the next 15 years” jobs in the service sector are probably the most at risk, with cashiers already being eliminated through self-checkout and now Amazon Go stores where you can scan products while you are shopping and just walk out. It will be interesting to look into how the automation of these day to day exercises could impact the structural framework of cities.

Uber, Zipcar sign car-sharing deal

USA Today

Will car ownership become a thing of the past?  Companies such as Zipcar allow people living in urban environments to avoid the hassles while enjoying the freedom associated with car ownership.  In many cities it is possible to get around without owning a car by utilizing public transportation and ridesharing services, but some tasks still require a personal vehicle.  Such services provide an alternative to car ownership.  A recent partnership between Zipcar and Uber has opened the door for drivers to not own their own vehicles.  Will these innovations positively impact overcrowding and parking shortages in dense locations?

the future of on-campus parking

New York Times

With the introduction of autonomous vehicles and rideshare services, parking garages may become a thing of the past.  This change in transportation structure will result in rethinking entry and circulation around campuses.  Will people enter on foot, or will services allow them to be dropped in the center of campus without worrying about the hassle of parking?  The University of Wisconsin-Madison is experiencing a parking shortage.  The campus is increasing its current parking capacity, but they are focused on creating flexible structures in anticipation for an automated future. By designing with future adaptation in mind, the new parking structures that are being built may one day be transformed into useful campus buildings when the need for parking declines.
